### What is a Convection Oven?

Convection ovens feature a built-in fan to circulate hot air and reduce moisture within the oven cavity. True convection ovens also feature an additional heating element. They can help promote the browning of meats, caramelized roasted vegetables and flaky baked goods.

### What is the Difference Between Convection and True Convection?

True convection features a third heating element at the back of the oven. This takes the fan from simply creating better airflow within the oven, to actively blowing hot air into the mix. The additional heat source contributes to temperature consistency and can help cook some dishes faster.

True convection is sometimes called European convection because it is more common in Europe. Brands may have their own unique name for this kind of convection oven but look for an indication of a third heating element to determine if it offers true convection.

### What Are the Advantages of Convection Ovens?

Convection ovens aid in browning and crisping for a variety of dishes, making it an ideal setting for roasted meats, flaky pastries and more. The circulating air means you may not need to rotate dishes during the cycle. The intense heat can also sometimes cook your food faster, depending on the recipe.

### How to Use a Convection Oven

Convection ovens may cook some foods faster so start by converting your recipe temperature, cook time, or both. You should also place your dishes in a way that allows for maximum airflow and check dishes early for doneness. Make sure you’re getting the most out of your convection system by following these three steps.

#### Adjust Recipes for Convection Ovens

Some recipes provide a convection oven conversion. If not, a general rule of thumb is to reduce the recipe temperature by 25 °F and begin checking for doneness with 20-25% of the time left. For example, if your recipe instructs you to bake at 350 °F for 60 minutes, you'll convert this to 325 °F and begin checking at 45 minutes. 

It is not necessary to reduce the temperature for meats or proteins so leave the temperature as is per your recipe and just make sure to look in on it sooner.

#### Keep an Eye on Dishes When First Using Convection Oven

When trying out a new recipe, it doesn’t hurt to turn on the oven light so you can keep an eye on your dishes. Using the light helps you avoid opening the door which can interfere with the temperature.

#### Position Dishes for Maximum Airflow

One of the main benefits of a convection oven is the circulation of hot air for even results. Here are some ways to take maximum advantage of this feature: 

- Use low-sided or perforated pans and dishes for better exposure to the heated air.

- Avoid placing dishes too close to heating elements. 

- Avoid overloading the oven or in a way that blocks the flow of air around dishes.

- Keep 3 inches of clearance between dishes and the outside oven walls.

### When to Use Convection Cooling

#### Roasting Meats

The circulating air helps crisp up skins more quickly giving you a crackly exterior in less time, so insides remain juicy. This same process helps you enhance browning, locking in flavourful juices on cut meats as well.

#### Roasting Vegetables Fruit And More

Convection ovens speed up the caramelization of sugars on the outside of ingredients for beautifully roasted carrots, parsnips, potatoes and more. Perfectly roasted vegetables let you add new depth to standards like hummus with this roasted cauliflower version or serve up a comforting bowl of roasted vegetable soup.

Sheet Pan Dinners

Since convection cooking is great for roasting meats and veggies, a sheet pan dinner that features both is an ideal weeknight dinner. The low-sided pan allows air to envelop your ingredients in just the right heat for incredible flavours and textures.

### Toasting Or Dehydrating

Convection cooking provides a drier environment than conventional cooking as it drives fast-moving, heated air directly over food surfaces. This makes it ideal for recipes involving dehydrating or toasting ingredients such as these layered goat cheese toasts.

### Light And Flaky Baked Goods

Pies, scones, flaky pastries, and other dense, non-yeast mixtures will benefit from the intense heat to maximize rise and golden browning.

### Multiple Batches Of Cookies And More

Convection ovens use a fan to drive hot air throughout the cavity so you can let multiple sheets of cookies bake up beautifully without rotating or shifting them around in the oven. This goes for multiple dishes in general, too. With even heat throughout the oven, you’ll get more consistent results when cooking on multiple racks.

### Casseroles And Pasta Bakes

The enhanced distribution of heated air in a convection oven makes it an ideal environment for most baked dishes that star a variety of ingredients like pasta, vegetables, sauces, meats, and cheese. Get a bubbly, just browned finish on cheese-topped dishes or a touch of caramelization on tender bakes like vanilla bean sweet potato gratin. 

### Air Frying

Air fryers are essentially mini convection ovens. This means, you can achieve similar results in your full-sized oven. Use a perforated tray or basket to air fry in your convection oven for a healthy take on an indulgent favourite. We even have an air frying 101 guide for all of your air frying needs!  Best of all, you can double your air fryer recipe for even more of your go-to snack.

### When to Use Conventional Cooking or Baking

#### Delicate Or Wet Mixtures

The movement of circulating air within a convection oven may disturb delicate bakes or very liquid mixtures like angel food cakes, custards, or souffles. These recipes are better off in the still baking environment of a conventional oven vs a convection oven.

#### Yeast Breads

Mixtures that rely on the expansion of air bubbles to rise also fair better in the stillness of a regular oven.

#### When You Are Unsure

Convection ovens may cook some dishes faster than conventional ovens. If your recipe is written for a conventional oven, you’ll have to reduce heat, time, or both to achieve optimal results in a convection oven. If your oven doesn’t automatically convert the settings for convection, and you’re unable to calculate it yourself, stick with regular cook settings. However, there are some basic rules you can follow for simple conversion. Read on to learn how.
